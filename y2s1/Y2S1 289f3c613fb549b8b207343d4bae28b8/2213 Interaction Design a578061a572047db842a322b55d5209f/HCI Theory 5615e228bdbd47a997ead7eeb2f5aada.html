Approaches to HCI Theory

Classical

In HCI, affordances are more often defined in terms of perception - i.e. how objects suggest possible uses.

Kaptelinin & Nardi

They propose a ‚Äúre-grounding‚Äù of affordance towards a mediational (acting through) view of interaction.

They propose six types of interaction:

  • Instrumental: A way in which a person can act through a tool to bring about change in the world, specifically by mediating between the user and an object.
    • An instrumental affordance can be decomposed into handling and effector affordances.
  • Handling: A handling affordance is a way in which a person can interact with the tool itself, like how a scroll bar lets you move a user interface.
  • Effector: An effector affordance is a way in which a tool can bring about change in an object - cutting a log, or sending a message.
  • Aggregation: Some artefacts can be combined with others to form a larger-scale compound mediator - mobile phone + headphones ‚Üí personal music player
  • Learning: Learning (which is itself a second-order mediation, apparently) is required in order to acces the other affordances of a tool. A learning affordance provides the opportunity to learn how the tool works.
  • Maintenance: Technology often requires maintenance and therefore must provide affordances for doing so. Examples include a mobile phone with the facility to change the battery or sim.
