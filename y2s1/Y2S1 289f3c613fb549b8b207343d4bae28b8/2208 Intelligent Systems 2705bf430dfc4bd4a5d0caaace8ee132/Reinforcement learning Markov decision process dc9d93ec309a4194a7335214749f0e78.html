In bandits, the agent received evaluative feedback based on their actions. However, there was no notion of state; the exploration of the space was simply to gain more data.

This is where the Markov decision process (MDP) comes in:

Flexible framework

Time steps don‚Äôt just have to be clock time - it could be stages of decisions or actions etc.

Actions can be low-level (turning a wheel, increasing voltage) or high-level (going shopping, doing coursework). It seems that they can also involve internal state (focusing attention, etc.).

Note that the environment is considered to be external to the agent.

The line between the agent and the environment is the agent‚Äôs limit of control, not the agent‚Äôs limit of knowledge.

States can be abstract (symbolic description) or concrete (sensor readings).

Summary - rational decision making

The objective of ‚Äúrational decision making‚Äù in this context is to maximise rewards (current and future). This tell us what we have to do, not how. Various approaches tell us how to maximise rewards.

Bandits have only action and reward.

Markov decision processes (MDP) have state, action and reward.

The environment is unknown, so we have to balance exploration and exploitation. We have Œµ\varepsilonŒµÔªø-greedy approaches to distribute the proportion of these goals.

The objective of reinforcement learning to is learn a policy - a set of state-action transitions.

The value function is given by the expected future rewards from every state given a policy. We can estimate this using TD(Œª\lambdaŒªÔªø) - temporal difference algorithm.

The idea of Q-learning is to assess the quality of an action instead of the possible reward of a state.

Apparently we do this to ease our calculations. We only have one function QQQÔªø to worry about and R(s,a)R(s,a)R(s,a)Ôªø is a quantified metric
