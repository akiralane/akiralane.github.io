Summary - rational decision making

In bandits, the agent received evaluative feedback based on their actions. However, there was no notion of state; the exploration of the space was simply to gain more data.

This is where the Markov decision process (MDP) comes in:

MDP is based on sequential decision making - actions (aaa﻿) influence immediate rewards and future states (sss﻿).

Like bandits, there’s a trade-off between delayed rewards and immediate rewards.

In bandits, you estimate q∗(a)q_*(a)q∗​(a)﻿ - whereas in MDP, you estimate q∗(s,a)q_*(s,a)q∗​(s,a)﻿.

The addition of state is the distinguishing factor.

Agent-environment interaction

The agent provides actions to the environment, which changes it somehow.

The environment provides state and reward back to the agent, which it uses to decide its next action.

This happens in discrete time steps t∈Nt\in \Nt∈N﻿.

In a finite MDP, the sets of states, actions and rewards (S,A,RS,A,RS,A,R﻿) are finite.

Here’s how the “dynamics” of MDP are defined:

p(s′,r∣s,a)=P(St=s′,Rt=r∣St−1=s,At−1=a)p(s',r|s,a)=P(S_t=s',R_t=r|S_{t-1}=s,A_{t-1}=a)p(s′,r∣s,a)=P(St​=s′,Rt​=r∣St−1​=s,At−1​=a)﻿

That is, the dynamic of going from sss﻿ to s′s's′﻿ as a consequence of aaa﻿ and receiving rrr﻿ is the probability that S=s′S=s'S=s′﻿ and R=rR=rR=r﻿ at time ttt﻿ given that S=sS=sS=s﻿ and A=aA=aA=a﻿ at time t−1t-1t−1﻿.

This is called Markovian dynamics, because StS_tSt​﻿ and RtR_tRt​﻿ depend only on St−1S_{t-1}St−1​﻿ and At−1A_{t-1}At−1​﻿. Again, there is no sense of memory.

Flexible framework

Time steps don’t just have to be clock time - it could be stages of decisions or actions etc.

Actions can be low-level (turning a wheel, increasing voltage) or high-level (going shopping, doing coursework). It seems that they can also involve internal state (focusing attention, etc.).

Note that the environment is considered to be external to the agent.

The line between the agent and the environment is the agent’s limit of control, not the agent’s limit of knowledge.

States can be abstract (symbolic description) or concrete (sensor readings).

Example: recycling robot

This is the robot which goes about picking up cans.

Rewards RRR﻿: mostly 0, small positive when a can is found and a large negative when the battery runs out. It should be balanced such that rsearch>rwaitr_{search}>r_{wait}rsearch​>rwait​﻿ so that the robot doesn’t just sit in one place.

States SSS﻿: {high battery, low battery}\{\text{high battery, low battery}\}{high battery, low battery}﻿

Actions AAA﻿: {wait, search, recharge}\text{\{wait, search, recharge\}}{wait, search, recharge}﻿

The transition from low → high via search is the case that the battery runs out while searching and a manual rescuer has to recharge them, hence the -3 reward.

This is opposed to the low → high via recharge, which has a reward of 0 - we don’t want to reward charging, we simply want to avoid running out of charge while searching.

Summary - rational decision making

The objective of “rational decision making” in this context is to maximise rewards (current and future). This tell us what we have to do, not how. Various approaches tell us how to maximise rewards.

Bandits have only action and reward.

Markov decision processes (MDP) have state, action and reward.

The environment is unknown, so we have to balance exploration and exploitation. We have ε\varepsilonε﻿-greedy approaches to distribute the proportion of these goals.

The objective of reinforcement learning to is learn a policy - a set of state-action transitions.

The value function is given by the expected future rewards from every state given a policy. We can estimate this using TD(λ\lambdaλ﻿) - temporal difference algorithm.

The idea of Q-learning is to assess the quality of an action instead of the possible reward of a state.

Apparently we do this to ease our calculations. We only have one function QQQ﻿ to worry about and R(s,a)R(s,a)R(s,a)﻿ is a quantified metric

Seems like you get VVV﻿ from TD-lambda and QQQ﻿ from Q-learning.
