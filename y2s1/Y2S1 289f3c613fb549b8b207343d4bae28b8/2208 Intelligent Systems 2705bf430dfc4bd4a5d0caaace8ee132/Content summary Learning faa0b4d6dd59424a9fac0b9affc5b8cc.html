Three types of learning:

Supervised learning is where we provide a labelled dataset for our model to learn from.

Image recognition, sentiment analysis, speech recognition.

Unsupervised learning is where the model finds its own patters from the data based on how its output is evaluated. We don’t know the correct answer.

Speech synthesis, “imagine pictures”, generate videos.

Reinforcement learning focuses on developing a policy of actions based on reward values it gets from undertaking said actions.

This involves multi-armed bandits, recycling-bot (for MDP)

  1. Start of iteration step: we have some sort of decision boundary, characterised by a perpendicular vector www﻿.
  1. Iteration step: adjust the decision boundary based on incorrectly categorised labels.
    1. The test for categorisation is y^=sign(wx)\hat{y}=\text{sign}(wx)y^​=sign(wx)﻿. This tells us whether our xxx﻿ is above or below the boundary. We check this against what we think the category should be and act accordingly.
    1. www﻿ becomes w±ηxw\pm\eta xw±ηx﻿ where:
      1. It’s plus or minus depending on whether we have to correct up or down.

The decision boundary is only a line in a two dimensional feature space - for higher dimensions, it becomes a (hyper)plane!

Hidden layers and representation learning

Perceptrons can linearly separate the outputs for OR and AND.

We can also map feature spaces which might not intially seem to be linearly separable by transforming them into higher dimensions and using hyperplanes.

Remember the example of how you could linearly separate the circle by mapping into 3D and then using original distance to the origin as the new yyy﻿-axis.

What about XOR, or other non-linearly separable functions?

Generally, we somehow map the space into some other state which is linearly separable, so that we can feed it into our perceptron.

To do this, we want a linear map plus some non-linear function.

This is usually done with a “hidden layer”:

H=σ+[WX]H=\sigma_+[WX]H=σ+​[WX]﻿

WWW﻿ is our linear map.

σ+\sigma_+σ+​﻿ is called the ReLU function (rectified linear unit) - which simply returns the input if it’s greater than zero and outputs zero otherwise.

This gives you that diode-esque graph.

Reinforcement learning: multi-armed bandits

The goal in reinforcement learning, as I have reiterated so many times now, is to develop a policy π\piπ﻿ to maximise total rewards.

A policy is a distribution of actions and is updated based on the reward observed after taking an action - bad or neutral rewards will decrease the probability of picking that action while good rewards will increase it.

We have a utility or state-value function V:S→RV:S\rarr \RV:S→R﻿ which tells us the desirability of being in a state s∈Ss\in Ss∈S﻿.

Notably, when we’re talking about basic multi-armed bandits, there is no notion of state. We simply explore the space of actions and their respective rewards - Markov decision processes are what add state.

We have an action-value function Q:A×S→RQ:A\times S\rarr \RQ:A×S→R﻿ which tells us the desirability of picking a certain action a∈Aa\in Aa∈A﻿ when in a given state sss﻿.

The reward we get from actions is nondeterministic - the outside environment is unpredictable. Hence, we use the notion of expectation to determine our value.

This means we need to consider future rewards.

The best action a∗a^*a∗﻿ is one which maximises expected utility: argmax EV(a)\text{argmax }\mathbb{E}V(a)argmax EV(a)﻿

argmax simply picks the argument which maximises the function.

We can use the idea of kkk﻿-armed bandits (that is, mutli-armed slot machines) as a model for this.

A big part of developing a policy is balancing exploration and exploitation.

Exploration as in deliberately picking unknown or bad options in order to gain more information about the space.

Exploitation as in using existing knowledge to pick the best option immediately available.

We don’t have precise information in the beginning, so it’s usually better to weight towards exploration at the start.
