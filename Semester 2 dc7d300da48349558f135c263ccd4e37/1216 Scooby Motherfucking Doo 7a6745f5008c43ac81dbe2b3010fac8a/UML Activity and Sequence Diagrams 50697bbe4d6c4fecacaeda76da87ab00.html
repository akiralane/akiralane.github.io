UML Activity and Sequence Diagrams

Activity diagrams

Activity Diagrams are the UML equivalent of a flow chart. They consist of activities linked by transitions with a single start and stop.

Decision points (branches) are written as diamonds. The "guard conditions" out of the branch are labelled, and the decision point has an optional decription.

Bars are used to show that shings happen in parallel, or to bring together several transitions.

Swimlanes partition a diagram to show different logical areas. This can be according to phase, actor, department, or some other category. There is no standard way - partition however is most useful.

When to use activity diagrams?

  • When analysing a single use case - what actions happen and when?
  • Useful for communicating order and dependency of actions

They do not convey interactions, data or the state of actors. Other UML diagrams do this.

Sequence diagram

Sequence diagrams consist of objects each with their own "lifeline".

A "message" can be sent between lifelines - this could represent a direct function call or activation of a listener.

Processes are denoted using long rectangles.

"Alt" frames denote two or more possible choices of message. They are the sequence diagram's if/elif/else statement.

"Opt" frames denote an optional message - a message may or not be send depending on the evaluation of a conditon.

"Par" frames denote the execution of parallel messages.

When to use sequence diagrams?

  • Also when analysing a single use case - except this time you want to focus on what the key roles are, how they interact and in what order.
  • Focus on Sequence of Interaction instead of Flow of Control
