However, in optimisation problems, we're less interested in constraints. If we're interested in finding the optimum, we can use the cost as a constraint!

Any partial solution has to have a lower cost than the best solution we've found so far.

This allows us to develop a backtracking stratgey known as branch and bound.

In practice, we can employ useful heuristics (see section lower in the page) to get a good starting bound (e.g. by using a greedy algorithm for the TSP).

Applications of Branch and Bound

Branch and bound works for many optimisation problems. Its drawback is that you often end up still searching an exponentially large search space, even if it's much less than a brute force enumeration.

Making it work well requires considerable effort; for really large problems, branch and bound may be too slow.

Heuristics

Given that there are currently no know efficient algorithms for finding optimal solutions to NP-Hard problems, we are left with:

  • spending potentially a very long time searching for an optimal solution (e.g. with branch and bound)
  • accepting "good" solutions which aren't necessarily optimal.

Algorithms for finding good soltuions are often called approximation algorithms or heuristic algorithms.

The idea behind heuristic algorithms is to use a rough guide or heuristic pointing us in a reasonable direction.

Two commonly used heuristics are:

  • A greedy heuristic - take the best move available
  • We choose to believe that good solutions are "close" to each other.

Heuristic algorithms fall broadly into two categories:

  • Constructive algorithms
  • Local/neighbourhood search

Constructive algorithms

Constructive algorithms build up a solution from scratch.

They usually rely on a greedy heuristic and are very fast - once they obtain a solution, they stop.

They can give reasonable solutions very quickly, but the quality of the solution is often not very good.

Neighbourhood search

Local search algorithms require some initial solution, often generated randomly.

  1. Start from an initial solution
  1. Examine the neighbouring solutions
  1. Move to a neighbour if it is better (or simply not worse)
  1. Repeat from step 2 until some stopping criterion is met.

These are known as hill-climbers or descent for maximisation and minimsation respectively.

Note that this only finds a local solution (as you'd expect).

Simulated annealing

This is an extension to neighbourhood search.

A stochastic optimisation method named in analogy to physical annealing:

A crystalline solid is heated and then left to slowly cool until it's free of crystal defects (i.e. it reaches its lowest crystal lattice energy state).

That is, we perturb our solution in the wrong direction deliberately in order to potentially find a better optimum.

This is often done randomly.
