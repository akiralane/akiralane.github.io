Linear Programming except more

We're looking at linear programs from a geometric angle.

The constraints in a Linear Program describe a convex polytope in nnnÔªø-dimensional space.

The objective functinon will attain its minimum/maximum at a vertex of the polytope; obviously, the optimum is never in the interior.

Our solution policy can therefore be to start at some vertex and move to a neighbouring vertex that gives the best improvement in cost.

When no further moves are possible, we're done; this is the Simplex Algorithm.

This means that roughly speaking, Simplex is an iterative improvement algorithm that traverses the vertices of the constraint polytope in search of a global optimum.

However, there's still a lot of work to realise this solution.

Weird Time Complexity of Simplex

It turns out that Simplex typically runs in O(n3)O(n^3)O(n3)Ôªø time in practice.

However, it is possible to create problems where there is a "long path" from the initial solution to the optimum, which is exponentially large.

Hence the theoretical worst-case of Simplex is exponential, although in reality this almost never happens.
